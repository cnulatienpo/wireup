How the system talks back

Until now, everything has stayed inside.

Images appeared in viewers. Numbers moved through wires. Sound pushed on visuals. Control came from hands on sliders and keys. All of it lived inside the same room.

Systems rarely stay contained.

Eventually something needs to leave. A signal gets recorded. A value gets sent. A decision gets exposed to another machine.

Start from a network where something meaningful is already happening.

Pick one signal you care about.

Output does not mean display. It means declaration.

Move your mouse to an empty area to the right of your network.

Press Tab.

Type one of the following and place it there:

  • Out (TOP, CHOP, or SOP)
  • Record
  • OSC Out
  • MIDI Out

Pick one that matches the kind of signal you want to send.

These nodes do not change the system. They expose it.

Wire the signal you chose into the output node.

Something subtle happens here.

The network does not behave differently, but its role changes.

It is no longer just reacting. It is announcing.

If you chose a recorder, let it run for a moment.

If you chose a network output, watch the activity indicators.

The system is now accountable.

Output nodes enforce consistency. They demand valid ranges, stable formats, and regular timing.

Anything sloppy upstream becomes obvious once it has to leave the room.

This is why output is never an afterthought.

Sending something outward forces the system to explain itself in a way viewers never require.

Many networks become simpler at this stage. Not because features are removed, but because only the parts that survive exposure matter.

The system is no longer just running. It is communicating.

What it says is exactly what you wired it to say.
